Classic Games and Their Lessons

These foundational games capture recurring strategic situations you face every day.

The Prisoner's Dilemma

Two suspects are interrogated separately. Each can cooperate (stay silent) or defect (betray the other). The rational choice for each individual is to defect, but mutual cooperation produces the best combined outcome.

Real-world examples: Price wars, arms races, climate negotiations, team projects. The dilemma appears whenever individual incentives conflict with collective welfare.

The solution? Repeated interaction, reputation, and commitment. KeepRule's cooperation rules help you build trust in repeated games.

Tit-for-Tat: The Winning Strategy

In repeated prisoner's dilemmas, the simplest strategy wins: cooperate first, then mirror whatever your opponent did. This strategy is nice (starts cooperating), retaliatory (punishes defection), forgiving (returns to cooperation), and clear (easy to understand). Build tit-for-tat rules at KeepRule.com.

The Chicken Game

Two drivers race toward each other. The first to swerve is the "chicken." Neither wants to swerve, but if neither does, both crash. This models brinkmanship in negotiations, territorial disputes, and workplace conflicts.

Key insight: Credible commitment to not swerving gives you leverage -- but carries catastrophic risk. KeepRule risk assessment helps you evaluate when to hold firm and when to yield.

The Stag Hunt

Two hunters can hunt a stag together (high reward, requires cooperation) or hunt hare alone (low reward, guaranteed). This is about trust and coordination, not conflict.

Key insight: The stag hunt shows why trust is so valuable. When you trust your partner to cooperate, both achieve more. KeepRule coordination tools build the trust infrastructure.

Battle of the Sexes

Nash Equilibrium in Daily Life

A Nash Equilibrium is a state where no player can improve their outcome by changing strategy alone. You encounter Nash Equilibria daily:

  • Traffic: Everyone drives on the same side of the road
  • Pricing: Competitors settle at similar price points
  • Social norms: Shaking hands, queuing, tipping
  • Technology: Everyone uses the same messaging platform

Understanding equilibria helps you predict behavior and find opportunities to shift to better equilibria. KeepRule strategic thinking tools map out these dynamics.

Schelling Focal Points

When players cannot communicate, they often coordinate on "obvious" choices. If asked to meet someone in New York City with no prior arrangement, most people choose Grand Central Station at noon.

Focal points emerge from shared culture, convention, and salience. In business, being the "obvious choice" is immensely valuable. KeepRule.com helps you identify and leverage focal points in strategic situations.

Mechanism Design

If game theory analyzes existing games, mechanism design creates new ones. It is the engineering side of strategic interaction.

What is Mechanism Design?

Mechanism design asks: "Given the outcome I want, what rules should I set?" It is reverse game theory. Instead of predicting behavior in a given game, you design the game to produce desired behavior. KeepRule.com applies mechanism design to personal decision systems.

Auction Theory

Auctions are mechanism design in action. Understanding auction types helps in bidding, selling, and negotiating.

English Auction

Ascending price. Bidders drop out as price rises. Winner pays just above second-highest bid. This is what most people picture as an "auction."

Dutch Auction

Descending price. Auctioneer lowers price until someone accepts. Rewards bold bidders but risks overpaying. Used for flowers, IPOs, and Treasury bills.

Sealed First-Price

Submit one bid in secret. Highest bid wins and pays their bid. Strategy: bid below your true value (shade your bid). Common in procurement and real estate.

Vickrey (Second-Price)

Submit one bid in secret. Highest bid wins but pays the second-highest bid. Optimal strategy: bid your true value. Elegant and truthful.

The Winner's Curse

In common-value auctions (where the item has the same value to all bidders but that value is uncertain), the winner tends to be the person who most overestimated the value. Winning feels good but often means you overpaid.

Protection strategies: Shade your bid downward, especially with many competitors. Set hard maximum prices before the auction begins. KeepRule pre-commitment rules prevent emotional overbidding.
